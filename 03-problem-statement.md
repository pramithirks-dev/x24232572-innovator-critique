# Problem Statement

## The Problem
Human–elephant conflict (HEC) is the single largest cause of unnatural elephant deaths and rural property damage in India. According to India's Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change, **over 500 people and 100+ elephants are killed every year** in HEC incidents, with annual crop losses exceeding ₹500 crore (~US $60 million). Smallholder coconut, banana, and paddy farmers along the **Western Ghats fringe — particularly in Wayanad (Kerala), Hassan (Karnataka), and the Coimbatore–Valparai belt (Tamil Nadu)** — bear the brunt. Existing mitigations are blunt: electric fences electrocute calves, firecrackers traumatise herds and trigger stampedes, and elephant-proof trenches collapse in monsoon. State compensation is slow (12–18 months), capped, and paid only after damage occurs.

## Narrow Customer Segment
**Smallholder coconut farmers (0.5–4 hectares) in the Western Ghats fringe districts of Kerala, Karnataka, and Tamil Nadu whose plots lie within 2 km of a recognised elephant corridor.**

- Population: ~180,000 households
- Average annual crop loss to HEC: ₹45,000–₹120,000 per holding
- Average state compensation received: ₹8,000–₹15,000 (after 14-month delay)
- Smartphone penetration: 78%
- Existing willingness-to-pay for night surveillance: ₹300–₹600/month (validated via Kudumbashree microfinance surveys, 2024)

## The Opportunity
A **non-lethal, AI-vision-based perimeter monitoring service** that detects approaching herds in real time, deploys species-appropriate acoustic deterrents, alerts the farmer's phone, and feeds anonymised herd movement data to forest departments — protecting both farmer livelihoods and a culturally revered keystone species.
